Hanukkah: Understanding the Jewish Festival of Lights and its Traditions

Hanukkah, often called the Jewish Festival of Lights, commemorates the rededication of the Second Temple in Jerusalem. The holiday celebrates the Maccabean Revolt against Greek-Syrian oppression and the miracle of a single day's worth of oil burning for eight days. Celebrated over eight days, a candle is lit each evening on a nine-branched candelabrum called a hanukkiah. The date of Hanukkah varies annually in the Gregorian calendar, beginning on the 25th day of the Hebrew month of Kislev. While gifts were not originally part of the tradition, they are now customary, especially for children. Traditional foods include deep-fried items like sufganiyot (jelly doughnuts) and latkes (potato pancakes). In 2025, Hanukkah begins on December 14.
