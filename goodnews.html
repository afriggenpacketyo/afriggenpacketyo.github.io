Charles H. Bennett and Gilles Brassard Awarded Turing Prize for Quantum Cryptography

2026-03-18 11:23:16 EDT

US physicist Charles H. Bennett and Canadian computer scientist Gilles Brassard have been awarded the Turing Award, known as the "Nobel Prize of computing," for their 1984 invention of quantum cryptography. Their groundbreaking work, based on quantum physics, has "redefined secure communication and computing" by developing a form of seemingly unbreakable encryption. Unlike current encryption reliant on complex mathematical combinations, their BB84 theory demonstrates that any attempt to hack or copy a quantum encryption key inherently changes the very behavior of its elements, making replication impossible. The award, which includes a $1 million prize, recognizes their foundational contribution to secure electronic communications, particularly in anticipation of the development of more powerful quantum computers.
