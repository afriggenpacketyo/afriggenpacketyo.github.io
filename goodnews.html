This essay explores how the Continental Army, under General George Washington, was instrumental in creating a unified American identity during the Revolutionary War. In 1775, the colonies lacked a shared culture, viewing each other as competitors rather than compatriots. The army brought together men from 13 different colonies, and their shared experiences of hardship and sacrifice on the battlefield blurred provincial loyalties and fostered new national bonds. The army also established unifying traditions, such as celebrating Washington's birthday instead of the King's and adopting a national flag.
