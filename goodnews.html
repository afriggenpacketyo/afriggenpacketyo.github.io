Study Reveals Great Apes Use Physical Contact for Trust and Conflict Resolution

2026-07-21 19:48:38 EDT

A study conducted by researchers at Durham University indicates that great apes, including both chimpanzees and bonobos, utilize physical contact like touching, embracing, and kissing as an ancient mechanism for building trust and de-escalating tension before stressful events. The research observed these behaviors, notably chimpanzees placing fingers in one another's mouths, serving as a "rallying cry" ahead of potential conflicts, such as those over food resources. Experts suggest that this social touch is one of the oldest forms of communication, likely predating language and originating at least seven million years ago, before humans and these apes diverged from a common ancestor. These findings imply that human gestures of reassurance, like hugs or pats on the shoulder before challenging situations, may draw upon a similarly ancient social toolkit.
