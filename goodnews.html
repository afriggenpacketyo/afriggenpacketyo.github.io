Study Reveals Great Apes Use Physical Contact to Build Trust Before Conflicts

2026-07-21 19:48:38 EDT

A study conducted by researchers at Durham University suggests that great apes, including chimpanzees and bonobos, utilize physical contact such as touching, embracing, and kissing to build trust and reassurance before stressful events. The experts observed this behavior, which even included chimpanzees placing fingers in one another's mouths, prior to conflicts over food at two great ape sanctuaries in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Zambia. Professor Zanna Clay described the behavior as "a rallying cry" before a stressful event, fostering a collective "we" rather than an individual "me." The findings indicate that the use of social touch to build trust and cooperation may be an ancient evolutionary trait, predating language by at least seven million years, shared by humans and their closest ape relatives.
