The bird, first domesticated in the Americas, was named by Europeans who confused it with the African guinea fowl, which was already known as a "turkey" because it was often imported through the Turkish region. When English colonists settled in North America, they brought the domesticated American turkey with them, solidifying its place in holiday traditions. Over time, the word also entered the English lexicon in negative colloquialisms like "cold turkey" or calling a failure a "turkey."
