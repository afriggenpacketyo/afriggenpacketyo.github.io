2025-07-27 07:30:00 EDT

The practice of tipping in the United States has its roots in the post-Civil War era, where it was used as a way for employers to avoid paying fair wages to formerly enslaved Black workers. This system evolved into the modern "tipped minimum wage," which under federal law is just $2.13 an hour, with the expectation that customer tips will make up the difference to the standard minimum wage. According to the Economic Policy Institute, this structure effectively shifts the responsibility of paying a worker's wage from the employer to the customer. This historical context explains why tipping is so ingrained in American service industries.
