2026-02-17 19:27:10 EST

Rev. Jesse Jackson's groundbreaking presidential campaigns in 1984 and 1988, though unsuccessful in securing the nomination, profoundly reshaped American presidential politics. He was the first Black candidate to appear on the ballot in all 50 states, mobilizing millions of Black voters and solidifying his influence within the Democratic Party. His critiques of delegate allocation rules led to significant reforms, including proportional representation, which later aided Barack Obama's 2008 victory. Jackson's campaigns are remembered as an "earthquake" that expanded the belief in what was politically possible for Black Americans.
