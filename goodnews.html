Catastrophe bonds are gaining popularity as a Wall Street financing tool designed to make insurance more affordable in communities facing high natural disaster risks. Institutional investors purchase these bonds, earning returns if a specified disaster doesn't occur, but their capital is redirected to insurers for claims if a qualifying event takes place. This mechanism provides insurers with crucial capital, especially as insured property losses have significantly increased, leading some companies to exit high-risk markets. An innovative model, exemplified in North Carolina, integrates bond proceeds into resilience projects like fortified roofs, demonstrating a proactive approach to both financial stability and disaster preparedness.
