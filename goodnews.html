2026-04-22 01:15:18 EDT

An amateur fossil hunter, Heather Salt, discovered a rare fragment of the upper jawbone from what is believed to be the world's oldest marine crocodile on Lyme Regis beach in Dorset. Initially mistaking the fossil for a piece of wood with nails, Salt realized its significance when shown to a fossil walk guide and later confirmed by a paleontologist. This find is considered one of only eleven such fossils known globally and is expected to aid researchers in understanding how this ancient creature hunted in early oceans. The fragment, part of the "Charmouth Crocodile" exhibit at Lyme Regis Museum, represents crucial evidence for the early evolution of crocodylomorphs.
