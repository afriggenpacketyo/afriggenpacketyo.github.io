The Philippines is facing challenges in meeting the global surge in demand for ube, a vibrant purple yam, due to a significant slowdown in domestic production. Despite its popularity in international cafes and bakeries, ube exports have increased, but local harvests have decreased substantially over the past two decades. Farmers cite a lack of planting materials as a major hurdle, forcing them to sell all their produce and rely on external sources for seedlings. The labor-intensive nature of ube cultivation and its long nine-month yield period, coupled with vulnerability to typhoons, makes it a less preferred crop compared to faster-maturing cash crops. Experts emphasize the need for government support in greenhouses and farmer training to ensure sustainable production.
