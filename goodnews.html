New York Times: Antibodies Identified for Universal Snake Antivenom

2025-05-02 16:31:48 EDT

New York Times reports that scientists have identified antibodies capable of neutralizing the venom of multiple deadly snake species, marking a significant step toward creating a universal antivenom. This breakthrough stems from the work of Tim Friede, who over 18 years injected himself with escalating doses of venom from 16 snake species, building immunity and allowing scientists to isolate powerful antibodies from his blood. These antibodies, combined with a drug that blocks neurotoxins, protected mice from the venom of 19 deadly snake species from various regions. This research offers hope for treating snakebites, which affect millions globally, particularly in poor rural areas with limited access to treatment, where current antivenoms are often species-specific and can cause severe allergic reactions.

Man Endures Snake Bites to Develop Broad Antivenom

2025-05-02 16:26:25 EDT

Tim Friede, fascinated by snakes since childhood, has willingly been bitten by venomous snakes approximately 200 times over nearly a quarter of a century, in an effort to develop immunity and contribute to the creation of a broad antivenom. In a new paper in the journal Cell, researchers say they've done just that, thanks in large part to the antibodies that Friede has developed over nearly a quarter century of self-envenomation. Researchers have identified antibodies in his blood that neutralize neurotoxins from multiple snake species, leading to a new antivenom cocktail that offers mice complete protection against 13 species and partial protection against six more. This research represents a significant step forward in addressing the global health problem of venomous snake bites, which cause tens of thousands of deaths and disabilities annually, particularly in low- and middle-income countries.
