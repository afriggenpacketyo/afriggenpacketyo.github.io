Historic Tradition of Walking Christmas Turkeys to London Markets Recalled

2025-12-24 00:32:22 EST

Long before modern transportation, thousands of turkeys, notably the Norfolk Black breed, were herded on foot for three months from East Anglia to London's Christmas markets in a historical practice known as turkey droving. Families like the Peeles, credited with preserving the Norfolk Black breed, recount how these birds, sometimes equipped with tiny leather boots or protective tar and sand coatings on their feet, would travel approximately three miles daily. Drovers would gather food along the route, with the turkeys typically roosting in trees overnight. This unique tradition, which supplied fresh poultry to wealthy Victorian Londoners, eventually ceased with the advent of steam engines and the expansion of railways in the late 1800s.
