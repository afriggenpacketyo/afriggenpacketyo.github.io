Ancient Native American Agricultural Site Discovered in Michigan

2025-06-06 06:00:00 EDT

Archaeologists have uncovered what is believed to be the largest intact remains of an ancient Native American agricultural site in the eastern United States, located in northern Michigan. Using drone technology, researchers mapped parallel rows of earthen mounds across 300 acres, revealing raised gardening beds used by ancestors of the Menominee Indian Tribe of Wisconsin to grow crops like corn, beans, and squash. The scale of the agriculture is surprising given the relatively poor growing conditions and the proximity of wild rice, raising questions about the community's motivations. The discovery highlights the extent of indigenous agriculture prior to European colonization and the sophisticated farming practices employed by Native American societies.

Ancient China Was Home to Earliest Known Matrilineal Society, Burials Indicate

2025-06-06 05:57:02 EDT

A team of scientists has discovered evidence of the world's earliest known matrilineal society in what is now Shandong, eastern China, dating back around 4,500 years. The community, which farmed millet and raised animals, maintained a stable lineage through the female line for at least 10 generations over 250 years. Researchers analyzed ancient DNA from 60 individuals to confirm the existence of this Neolithic society that traced descent through the maternal line. The findings support the hypothesis that early matrilineal systems could emerge and persist in communities without strong mechanisms for wealth accumulation, shedding light on early human social structures.
