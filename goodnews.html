Old Photo Aids Identification of Korean War Soldier's Remains After Modern Forensics Fail

2026-02-27 10:06:13 EST

The remains of U.S. Army Sgt. Roger Duquesne, a Korean War soldier reported missing in action since September 1950, were identified through the use of craniofacial superimposition with an old photograph, after newer forensic techniques like familial DNA matching proved unsuccessful. Duquesne's remains, initially buried as an unknown at the National Memorial Cemetery of the Pacific in 1956, were disinterred in 2011 for further examination. DPAA investigators, with specialized expert assistance, meticulously matched Duquesne's smiling photograph, particularly focusing on his prominent upper canine tooth and its unique shadow, to the recovered skull. This remarkable case serves as a clear example of how refined older forensic methods, when thoughtfully applied alongside historical records, can ultimately provide clarity and answers decades later, offering crucial closure to the families of fallen soldiers when other identification methods are exhausted.
