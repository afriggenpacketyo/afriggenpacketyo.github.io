2026-02-19 00:07:07 EST

The British Geological Survey later identified the find as a larger, columnal part of a crinoid, an ancient marine animal that first appeared over 500 million years ago. The fossil's distinctive mouth-like shape resulted from the lengthwise splitting and curving of its stem ossicles. The article explains that crinoids, often referred to as "sea lilies," are among the oldest complex animals, with smaller, individual discs from their stems commonly known as "St Cuthbert's beads" in the region.
