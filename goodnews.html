Archaeological Discovery Reveals Earliest Evidence of Human Fire-Making 400,000 Years Ago

2025-12-11 06:00:00 EST

A groundbreaking study published in the journal Nature presents the earliest evidence of humans making fire, discovered at a 400,000-year-old site in eastern Britain, pushing back the known timeline by over 350,000 years. Archaeologists found fragments of iron pyrite alongside fire-cracked flint handaxes in what appears to be a hearth, strongly suggesting early humans intentionally brought these raw materials to the site with the purpose of starting fires. This discovery is considered the most exciting of lead archaeologist Nick Ashton's 40-year career, as previous theories suggested early humans could only harness natural fires. The ability to make fire is recognized as one of the most important discoveries in human history, enabling protection from predators, enhanced nutrition from cooked food, settlement in inhospitable climates, and serving as a catalyst for social and behavioral evolution, including language and storytelling. Experts believe these fires were likely made by early Neanderthals, with the knowledge potentially spreading or being discovered independently by different groups over time.
