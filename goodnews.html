The discovery at Barnham, near a Neanderthal habitation site, includes a patch of heated clay, heat-shattered handaxes, and two pieces of imported iron pyrite, strongly suggesting intentional fire creation. This finding significantly pushes back the timeline for controlled fire use, which previously dated to about 50,000 years ago. Controlled use of fire was crucial for human evolution, enabling vastly improved diets through cooking, survival in colder climates, and fostering social development around campfires, which contributed to early forms of language and storytelling.
