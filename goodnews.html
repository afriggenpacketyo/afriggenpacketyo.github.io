Raymond Chandler's Battle Over a Split Infinitive with The Atlantic

2026-03-12 20:28:00 EDT

In 1948, renowned detective novelist and screenwriter Raymond Chandler engaged in a notable dispute with The Atlantic's editorial department over a copy editor's correction of a split infinitive in his essay. Chandler famously insisted on his unique, "broken-down patois," asserting his right to intentionally split infinitives and integrate barroom vernacular into his writing. The copy editor, Margaret Mutch, became the target of his literary ire, inspiring a mocking poem, "Lines to a Lady With an Unsplit Infinitive." This incident underscores Chandler's broader frustration with perceived arbitrary strictures, drawing parallels between his grievances against grammatical rules and his disdain for Hollywood's perceived embrace of mindless mediocrity.
