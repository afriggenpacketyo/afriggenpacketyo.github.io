France Symbolically Repeals Slavery-Era Legislation to Address Colonial Legacy

2026-05-28 12:02:00 EDT

French lawmakers have voted to formally repeal slavery-era legislation, including the "Code noir," a series of royal edicts from 1685-1724 that defined enslaved people as "movable property" and justified their abuse. While slavery was definitively abolished in France in 1848, this symbolic legislative action aims to address the country's complex colonial legacy and requires the government to report on slavery's lasting effects on racism and discrimination. Though the current bill does not include proposals for reparations, a contentious issue, it represents a powerful act of remembrance, justice, and recognition, aiming to heal historical wounds and ensure the history of slavery is taught in schools.

Supreme Court Overturns Death Row Conviction Citing Racial Bias in Jury Selection

2026-05-28 11:58:43 EDT

The Supreme Court ruled 5-4 in favor of Terry Pitchford, a Black death row inmate from Mississippi, overturning his capital murder conviction due to racial discrimination during jury selection. The majority opinion, authored by Justice Brett Kavanaugh and joined by Chief Justice Roberts, found that the trial court failed to afford Pitchford's counsel a sufficient opportunity to rebut the prosecutor's proffered race-neutral reasons for striking four of five potential Black jurors. This decision invalidates Pitchford's conviction, allowing the state to potentially retry him, and highlights the importance of adhering to the Batson v. Kentucky framework to prevent discriminatory jury practices.
