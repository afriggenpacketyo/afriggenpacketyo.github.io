Alan Greenspan, who served as Federal Reserve Chairman for nearly two decades, significantly expanded the institution's power and redefined its approach to monetary policy. He transitioned the Fed from monetarism to data dependence and Fed funds targeting, meticulously using economic indicators to set interest rates. Greenspan's era saw the Fed act as a crucial liquidity provider, earning the nickname "Greenspan put." Despite his intellectual opposition to big government, his leadership oversaw massive growth in the Fed's staff and its involvement in international banking standards.
