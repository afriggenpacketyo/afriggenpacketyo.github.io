Although its sale is illegal, demand remains high, and it is still widely available in markets. Biologists warn that the cactus grows extremely slowly, making commercial farming unviable and wild harvesting unsustainable. Chefs and conservationists are now advocating for substitutes to preserve both Mexico's rich culinary heritage and its unique biodiversity.
