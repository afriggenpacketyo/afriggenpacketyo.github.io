Ancient Chinese Star Catalogue May Be Oldest in the World

2025-05-05 03:32:35 EDT

Two Chinese scientists propose that "Shi's Star Catalogue," an astronomical list of celestial bodies, is the world's oldest star log, dating back approximately 2,400 years. Published in the journal Research in Astronomy and Astrophysics, the study cites new evidence suggesting the catalogue was created around 335BC, predating the Hipparchus star catalogue from Greece. The researchers used image processing technology to convert ancient records into measurable celestial coordinates, confirming observation years in the catalogue's main content, followed by revisions around AD125. This research suggests that detailed astronomical studies began in China and Greece around the same time, challenging the previously held belief that the Greek catalogue was the oldest.

Roses Were Likely Originally Yellow, Study Suggests

2025-05-05 01:00:09 EDT

A new genomic study by Chinese and Dutch researchers indicates that the first roses were likely yellow, contrary to the popular association of roses with red. The study, published in Nature Plants, analyzed domesticated and wild rose samples, revealing that their common ancestor probably had single-petal flowers with a yellow color and seven leaflets. Roses are among the most extensively cultivated ornamental flowers, known for their variety of shapes, sizes, and colors, but the ancestral traits suggest a different origin than the multilayered, pink and red roses commonly seen today.
