The article explores the historical origins of celebrating the New Year on January 1st, primarily attributed to the widely adopted Gregorian calendar, which evolved from ancient Roman systems. Initially, Roman calendars aimed to align lunar and solar cycles, leading to the addition of January and its designation as the year's start. Pope Gregory XIII later refined Julius Caesar's 365.25-day calendar with leap year rules, establishing the Gregorian calendar's global prevalence due to European economic dominance. While many cultures have their own new year celebrations, January 1st serves as a universal "fresh start" psychologically, fostering optimism for new beginnings.

The article delves into the science behind the aurora borealis and australis, explaining that these vibrant light displays are caused by solar storms ejecting charged particles that collide with Earth's atmospheric atoms and molecules. Different atmospheric elements, like oxygen (green) and nitrogen (purple/blue), determine the aurora's colors, with rare interactions creating scarlet red. While typically visible near the poles, recent powerful solar maximums have expanded their visibility to lower latitudes.
