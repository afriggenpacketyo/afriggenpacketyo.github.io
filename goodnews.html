2025-08-04 09:17:00 EDT

An international team of scientists is studying a 1.2-million-year-old ice core extracted from Antarctica, the oldest continuous sample ever drilled. This ancient ice acts as a climate archive, containing trapped air bubbles that reveal atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations from the distant past. Researchers hope the core will help explain a major shift in Earth's ice age cycles, from occurring every 40,000 years to every 100,000 years. Understanding this transition could improve climate models and provide better predictions of how the planet will respond to current, human-driven increases in CO2 levels, which are already far higher than any period recorded in the ice.
