South African Scientists Use Radioactive Isotopes to Combat Rhino Poaching

Scientists in South Africa have launched a novel anti-poaching initiative called the Rhisotope Project, which involves injecting rhino horns with harmless radioactive material. The process, developed at the University of the Witwatersrand, makes the horns easily detectable by radiation scanners at borders and airports, even in small quantities or inside large shipping containers. The goal is to deter poachers and traffickers by making the horns a high-risk, low-reward commodity. After successful trials confirmed the method is safe for the animals, the project officially launched with the injection of five rhinos, aiming to expand the program to protect the country's declining population.
