Technological Advances Have Made Computers Cheaper, But Moore's Law May Be Reaching Its Limit

2025-12-04 05:00:00 EST

Despite widespread inflation, the price of computers and other electronics has generally decreased over time, a phenomenon largely attributed to Moore's Law. Coined by Intel co-founder Gordon Moore, the principle observes that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles roughly every two years, leading to more powerful and less expensive computing. For example, an entry-level MacBook Pro today costs $200 less than its less powerful equivalent from five years ago. However, experts now warn that Moore's Law is slowing as transistors approach physical limits, meaning future gains in performance and price reduction may depend on innovations in software and chip packaging rather than miniaturization alone.
