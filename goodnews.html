Fossil Discovery in China Suggests Ancient Marine Animals Practiced 'Social Distancing'

2025-07-30 00:39:15 EDT

Chinese paleontologists have discovered that an ancient, clam-like marine invertebrate may have used bristle-like structures for "social distancing" to enhance its survival. The 436-million-year-old brachiopod fossils, found in China's Guizhou province, were arranged in a highly regular, checkerboard-like pattern on the former sea floor. Researchers believe the extinct species, Nucleospira calypta, used hundreds of flexible setae, each thinner than a human hair, to maintain optimal spacing from its neighbors. This behavior likely helped ensure each individual had adequate room for filter feeding, demonstrating a sophisticated survival strategy among early marine life.
