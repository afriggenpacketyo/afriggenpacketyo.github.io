SoCal Beetle's Ant Mimicry Offers Insights into Evolutionary Entrenchment

2026-02-10 14:22:10 EST

Caltech biologists studying the velvety tree ant in the Angeles National Forest have uncovered a rove beetle species, Sceptobius lativentris, that mimics ants, offering new insights into evolutionary "entrenchment." The beetle turns off its own pheromones and smears itself with ant pheromones to integrate into colonies, where it is fed and consumes ant eggs. This chemical mimicry, however, prevents the beetle from producing its own protective waxy barrier, making it dependent on the ants for survival and unable to leave the colony. This "Catch-22" mechanism provides a model for how intimate symbiotic relationships can become evolutionarily irreversible.
