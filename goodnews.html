Great Apes Use Physical Contact to Build Trust Before Conflicts

2026-07-21 19:48:38 EDT

A Durham University study reveals that chimpanzees and bonobos use touching, embracing, and kissing before stressful events, like food conflicts, to build trust and reassurance. Researchers observed chimpanzees engaging in close contact, including placing fingers in each other's mouths, a surprising finding given their aggressive reputation. This behavior, described as a "rallying cry," suggests social touch is an ancient form of communication, likely predating language. Scientists propose this trust-building mechanism may date back at least seven million years, indicating a deep evolutionary root for such cooperative behaviors in both apes and humans.
