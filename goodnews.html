2026-02-05 06:00:00 EST

New research on Nile crocodile bones suggests that previous estimations of dinosaur ages based on growth rings might be overestimates. Paleobiologist Anusuya Chinsamy-Turan and biologist Maria Eugenia Pereyra found more growth rings than expected in two-year-old crocodiles, indicating that these rings are not always formed annually. This finding challenges the long-held assumption in paleontology, implying that some dinosaurs may have reached adult size at a younger age than scientists once thought. The study serves as a "cautionary tale" for interpreting bone tissue, highlighting the need for more ground-truthing with modern animals to better understand dinosaur growth patterns and refine our knowledge of prehistoric life.
