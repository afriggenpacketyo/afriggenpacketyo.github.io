Specific fruits and vegetables recommended for optimal heart health, study suggests

2026-06-08 04:00:13 EDT

A new study indicates that consuming specific types of fruits and vegetables, rather than just any five portions daily, may be more beneficial for heart health due to higher flavanol content. Researchers suggest that blueberries, plums, blackberries, broad beans, and cherries, along with green tea, are ideal for increasing flavanol intake. The study, involving 30,000 participants, found that even those who regularly ate five portions of fruit and vegetables often lacked sufficient flavanols. These antioxidants are believed to support heart health, circulation, and blood vessel elasticity by reducing inflammation. While the NHS does not set a specific flavanol target, approximately 500mg daily is considered beneficial. The article lists foods with high flavanol content per portion, such as plums, cranberries, and blackberries. However, other experts urge caution, noting that more research is needed to confirm if increased flavanol levels directly prevent heart problems. The variability in flavanol content due to growing conditions, ripeness, and processing is also highlighted.

Chinese scientists engineer cotton with desert moss gene to resist fungus and increase yield

2026-06-08 04:00:12 EDT

A Chinese research team has successfully engineered a genetically modified cotton plant by introducing a gene from desert moss. This modification significantly enhances the plant's resistance to Verticillium wilt, a fungal disease often referred to as "cotton cancer," which can also affect other crops. The transgenic cotton demonstrated an approximate 23.8% increase in yield compared to non-transgenic varieties when exposed to the pathogen. This breakthrough offers a promising strategy for improving crop resilience against devastating diseases. The research, published in the Plant Biotechnology Journal, suggests that using genes from organisms adapted to extreme environments is a viable method for crop enhancement. Xinjiang, China's largest cotton-producing region, is undergoing desert transformation for agricultural purposes, making this development particularly relevant for increasing crop output and sustainability in the area.

Foreign investors divest billions from Korean stocks despite market rally, driven by portfolio rebalancing

2026-06-08 03:39:36 EDT

Foreign investors have divested billions of dollars from South Korean stocks this year, even as the Kospi index has achieved record gains, making it one of the world's top performers. On Monday alone, overseas investors sold approximately 1.24 trillion won (about $801 million) worth of Kospi shares. Analysts suggest this selling is not due to deteriorating fundamentals but rather a consequence of the market's success. As Korean stocks have surged, their weightings in global and emerging-market benchmarks have increased, forcing active fund managers to trim positions to comply with portfolio and risk limits. This phenomenon is described as "forced selling." The rapid ascent of Korean equities has created structural pressures for international investors, with some encountering regulatory limits on ownership of individual companies. Despite foreign outflows, domestic buying, particularly from retail investors, has largely offset these sales. The selling also reflects concerns about risk concentration, as the rally has become heavily reliant on Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix. However, market veterans maintain that the underlying fundamentals of Korean equities remain robust.
