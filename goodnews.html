Political Scientists Coin 'Dummymander' for Gerrymandering That Backfires on Its Architects

2026-02-23 17:04:00 EST

Partisan gerrymandering, the controversial practice of drawing electoral districts to unfairly favor one political party, possesses a peculiar and often satisfying weakness known as the "dummymander." This term, coined by political scientists Bernard Grofman and Thomas L. Brunell, describes instances where a gerrymander inadvertently backfires, ultimately harming the very party that designed it. This phenomenon can occur when a party, in its attempt to maximize seats, spreads its voters too thinly across multiple districts, making its candidates vulnerable in the event of a strong opposing political wave. Recent aggressive redistricting efforts, particularly by Republicans following former President Trump's urging, are now being closely watched for potential dummymanders in the upcoming midterms, highlighting the unpredictable and sometimes self-defeating nature of political mapmaking. This article suggests that voters might have a chance to give gerrymanderers their comeuppance.
