This effect describes a secondary electronic recoil when a neutral particle, such as dark matter, collides with an atomic nucleus, generating a detectable signal. The team from the University of the Chinese Academy of Sciences utilized cutting-edge equipment to confirm this long-standing theory, marking a significant step forward in the quest to detect dark matter. This invisible, gravitationally interacting component remains one of the most profound unsolved mysteries in modern physics, and this observation offers a new pathway for its potential discovery.
