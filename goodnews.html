The decision was driven by rising production costs, with each penny now costing 3.69 cents to make, and the declining use of cash in consumer transactions. While the penny will remain legal tender, the circulating supply will gradually diminish, and many retailers are expected to begin rounding cash transactions to the nearest nickel, a practice already adopted in Canada. Financial planners suggest that now is a good time for consumers to cash in their accumulated coins, as their purchasing power erodes due to inflation and exchanging them becomes less convenient.
