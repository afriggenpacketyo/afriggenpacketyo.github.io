Research Suggests Malaria Significantly Shaped Early Human Settlement in Sub-Saharan Africa

2026-04-23 11:50:18 EDT

New research indicates that mosquito-borne malaria played a profound role in shaping early human settlement patterns across sub-Saharan Africa for tens of thousands of years. Published in the journal Science Advances, the study employed climate models to identify historical malaria hotspots, correlating these with archaeological evidence of human habitation. The findings suggest that human populations largely avoided these disease-prevalent regions until approximately 15,000 years ago, when the sickle cell anemia mutation arose in West Africa, providing crucial protection against the illness. This genetic adaptation subsequently enabled human expansion into previously inhospitable areas, underscoring disease as a powerful, yet often overlooked, force in human evolution and geographical distribution.
