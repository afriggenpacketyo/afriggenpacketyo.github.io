The Historical Reasons Easter Remained a Primarily Religious Holiday

2026-04-05 06:00:00 EDT

This article explores why Easter, despite its profound theological importance, never achieved the same widespread secular cultural status as Christmas in North America and Europe. Early American settlers, particularly Puritans, objected to both holidays as times of excessive revelry, but Christmas was later successfully reinvented in the 19th century as a bourgeois, child-centered family holiday through popular literature. Easter, however, with its more challenging theological themes of death and resurrection, proved harder to secularize. Its supernatural elements remained front and center, preventing a similar cultural proliferation and maintaining its distinct religious focus, unlike the adaptable Christmas narrative.
