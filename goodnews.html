Ancient Cycad Plants Attract Nocturnal Pollinators by Producing Infrared Heat, Study Finds

2025-12-11 14:30:37 EST

New research published in Science reveals that some of the earliest plants, known as cycads, attracted nocturnal pollinators by generating heat, causing them to glow with infrared light. This mechanism, predating brightly colored flowers, allowed ancient cycads to signal pollen readiness to insects like beetles, which possess specialized heat-sensing antennae. Experiments using 3-D printed simulated cones confirmed that infrared glow alone was sufficient to attract hundreds of pollinating beetles. This discovery suggests infrared radiation may be the oldest discovered pollination signal, highlighting an evolutionary dance between plants and pollinators that shifted from heat-sensing to vision-based signals as new insect groups emerged.
