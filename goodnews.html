Berlin's Jewish Hospital: A Story of Survival Through the Holocaust

2026-05-26 12:49:00 EDT

Berlin's Jewish Hospital, founded in 1756, uniquely survived the Holocaust, operating throughout the Nazi era and continuing to function today. The hospital, initially open to all, faced restrictions after 1933, barred from treating "Aryan" patients and forced to dismiss non-Jewish staff. Walter Lustig, a controversial figure, headed the hospital during deportations, with some accounts suggesting he protected individuals while others criticize his collaboration. The hospital served as a transit camp and shelter for orphans, with a dramatic incident in the war's final days saving inmates from execution. Its survival is attributed to a complex interplay of bureaucratic interests and specific circumstances rather than overt resistance.
