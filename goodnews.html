Psychologist distinguishes hope from optimism, advocates for cultivation

2026-04-12 08:00:00 EDT

Psychology professor Jamil Zaki differentiates between optimism, defined as the belief that the future will be good, and hope, which acknowledges present difficulties but maintains a belief in the possibility of improvement through action. Zaki argues that cynicism is often mistakenly equated with wisdom, but research shows cynics are not smarter and are worse at discerning truth. He emphasizes that hope is an active, stubborn sense of the world that involves envisioning a better future and possessing the "willpower" and "waypower" to pursue it, often through community action. While genetics play a minor role, experience, particularly early life nurturing, significantly influences hopefulness. Zaki suggests cultivating hope through a "practice of noticing" the beautiful aspects of life, balancing negativity with attention to positivity, and engaging in hobbies that foster connection and joy. He highlights humanity's resilience through historical challenges, underscoring the importance of collective action and mutual support in fostering hope.
