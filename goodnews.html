2025-11-07 15:42:44 EST

James D. Watson, the pioneering scientist who, at just 24, co-discovered the double-helix structure of DNA in 1953, has died at the age of 97. His monumental breakthrough, recognized with a 1962 Nobel Prize alongside Francis Crick and Maurice Wilkins, fundamentally reshaped fields ranging from medicine and crimefighting to genealogy and ethics by revealing how genetic information is stored and replicated. However, Watson's later life was marred by a series of controversial and racially offensive remarks, for which he faced widespread condemnation and professional censure, including the revocation of honorary titles from his former research lab. His complex legacy thus intertwines unparalleled scientific achievement with deeply regrettable social views that he maintained until his death.
