Despite widespread inflation, the price of computers and other electronics has generally decreased over time, a trend largely attributed to Moore's Law. This principle, observed by Intel co-founder Gordon Moore, states that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every two years, making computing power progressively cheaper and more powerful. For example, an entry-level MacBook Pro today costs $200 less than its less powerful equivalent from five years ago. However, experts warn that Moore's Law is reaching its physical limits, meaning the era of predictably cheaper and faster electronics may be coming to an end without new technological breakthroughs.
