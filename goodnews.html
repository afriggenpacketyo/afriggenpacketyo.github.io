2025-05-17 16:38:36 EDT

Paleontologists have discovered the fossilized remains of a 500-million-year-old, three-eyed predator named Mosura fentoni in the Burgess Shale of the Canadian Rockies. The creature, nicknamed the "sea moth," was about the length of an index finger and had a circular mouth lined with teeth. The discovery provides new insights into animal life during the Cambrian period and reveals that Mosura was more complex than previously known radiodonts. The fossil showed the Mosura's open circulatory system and segmented body, indicating specialized functions.
