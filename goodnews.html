The Historical Reasons Behind Turkey's Prominence at Thanksgiving

2025-11-26 06:45:00 EST

The turkey became a holiday staple much later, largely due to practical and cultural reasons during the 19th century. The bird was abundant, relatively cheap, and large enough for festive gatherings, making it an accessible centerpiece for many families. Its status was cemented when President Abraham Lincoln declared Thanksgiving a national holiday in 1863, a move championed by magazine editor Sarah Hale, and was further promoted by the industrialization of food production.

Examining the Linguistic Origins and Cultural Evolution of the Word 'Turkey'

2025-11-26 05:00:00 EST

The name of the turkey, the bird central to Thanksgiving, has a confusing and uncertain history rooted in a case of mistaken identity. When the North American bird, Meleagris gallopavo, was introduced to Europe in the 16th century, it was confused with the African guinea fowl, which was already known as a "turkey" because it was imported via Turkish trade routes. The American bird eventually inherited the name. Over time, the word has also entered the English lexicon in various colloquialisms, such as "cold turkey" for quitting suddenly and "talking turkey" for speaking frankly, reflecting the bird's enduring, if sometimes negative, cultural presence.
