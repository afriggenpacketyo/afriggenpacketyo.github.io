Kenyan Cash Grant Experiment Halves Child Deaths, But Cost-Effectiveness Questioned

2025-08-22 07:15:00 EDT

A significant study by the charity GiveDirectly found that one-time cash grants of about $1,871 to over 10,500 households in rural Kenya reduced infant and child mortality by 48%. This impressive outcome, primarily seen shortly after the 2015-2017 dispersal, is attributed partly to increased hospital deliveries and improved food security, allowing mothers more time off work. However, the article highlights a crucial caveat: the program's cost-effectiveness for saving lives was not high, costing nearly $300,000 per life saved, which is less efficient than other interventions like Vitamin A supplementation or malaria prevention. The findings suggest that while cash transfers effectively reduce poverty, their life-saving impact, though substantial, may not be the most cost-effective approach for health.
