The seemingly coincidental German celebrations of St. Martin's Day and the start of Carnival on November 11 share a common historical origin. St. Martin's Day, commemorating the charitable saint, traditionally marked the end of the agricultural year with a feast before a period of fasting. This pre-Advent fast, similar to Lent, began after November 11. Consequently, the date became the last opportunity for exuberant celebration before a period of abstinence, giving rise to the modern Carnival festivities that kick off at 11:11 a.m., blending solemn remembrance with joyful revelry.
