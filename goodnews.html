Ants Architecturally Adapt Nests to Mitigate Disease Transmission

2025-10-21 07:13:58 EDT

A new study reveals that black garden ants actively redesign their nests to slow the spread of a lethal fungus, a phenomenon termed "architectural immunity." When exposed to the pathogen, ant colonies construct nests that are more compartmentalized, with fewer interconnections and more widely spaced entrances. These structural modifications complement their known social distancing behaviors, such as self-isolation by infected ants, to effectively protect the colony's most valuable members, like the queen and larvae. Scientists suggest these findings demonstrate a sophisticated form of collective social immunity and could offer principles for designing human public spaces that better prevent the spread of infectious diseases.
