Report Highlights High Fiscal Costs of Urban Sprawl for American Cities

2026-06-03 08:30:00 EDT

A new report by the Pew Charitable Trusts and other organizations argues that American cities are incurring excessive fiscal costs due to sprawling housing development. The research, simulating various construction scenarios across ten states, found that "infill" development—building within existing communities—requires approximately $21,000 less in upfront infrastructure expenses per home and 50% less in ongoing maintenance compared to urban fringe development. Additionally, infill generates about 13% more in property taxes per acre. This analysis underscores how current land-use policies, favoring single-family homes in low-density areas, lead to unsustainable financial burdens, advocating for denser, more fiscally efficient growth to maintain city solvency and potentially stabilize property taxes.
