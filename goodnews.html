Authorized Users Generally Not Liable for Unpaid Credit Card Debt

2026-07-20 11:01:05 EDT

An authorized user on a credit card is typically not legally responsible for the primary cardholder's unpaid debt. Unlike joint account holders, authorized users do not sign the account agreement and have no contractual obligation to the issuer. While debt collectors may attempt to contact authorized users, they generally lack legal grounds to sue them directly. It is crucial to distinguish between authorized users and joint account holders, as the latter share equal legal responsibility. Early intervention and exploring debt relief options are recommended if the primary cardholder struggles with payments.

Executor Responsible for Notifying Creditors After Borrower's Death

2026-07-20 09:56:20 EDT

Following a borrower's death, the executor or personal representative of the estate is typically responsible for notifying creditors and debt collectors. This involves identifying debts, providing proof of authority, and managing claims against the estate's assets. While creditors may learn of a death through other channels, the executor holds the legal duty to handle financial obligations. Surviving family members are generally not required to notify creditors unless they serve as the estate representative, though exceptions exist for joint account holders or co-signers who remain legally responsible for specific debts.
