Scientists have developed a potentially universal antivenom using antibodies extracted from the blood of Tim Friede, a man who intentionally subjected himself to hundreds of snake bites over 18 years. Friede's self-immunization process made him "hyper-immune" to snake neurotoxins, enabling researchers to isolate antibodies effective against 19 of the world's deadliest snakes. The new antivenom, proven protective in mouse trials against venom from black mambas, king cobras, and others, offers hope for a broad-spectrum antidote that could be administered in rural settings via a "venom EpiPen." The researchers plan to expand trials to treat dogs and create antivenom for viper bites.
