Decades-Old '85% Rule' for Setting U.S. Speed Limits Faces Re-evaluation

2025-08-17 00:00:53 EDT

A long-standing principle in U.S. traffic engineering, the '85% rule,' is being reconsidered by some states and federal agencies amid rising road fatalities. The rule suggests that speed limits should be set at the speed that 85% of drivers naturally travel, assuming drivers instinctively choose the safest pace. However, critics argue this creates a feedback loop where speeding leads to higher limits, and it fails to account for pedestrians and cyclists in urban areas. The Federal Highway Administration has clarified that this is a guideline, not a strict rule, encouraging communities to also consider road usage, crash data, and pedestrian safety when setting limits.

At low speeds, the wing is perpendicular to the fuselage for maximum lift, but as it approaches Mach 5, it pivots to become parallel, merging with the aircraft's body to function as a 'waverider.'
