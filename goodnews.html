The 436-million-year-old brachiopod fossils, found in Guizhou province, were arranged in a distinct checkerboard-like pattern on the former sea floor. Researchers believe the creatures, known as Nucleospira calypta, used hundreds of thin, bristle-like structures called setae to sense their neighbors and maintain optimal spacing. This regular arrangement likely provided each individual with adequate space for filter feeding, a behavior analyzed through advanced imaging and spatial analysis of the exceptionally preserved fossils.
