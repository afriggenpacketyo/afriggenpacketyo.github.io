Clarifying Bank Account Levies: Debt Collectors' Powers After Seven Years

2026-03-19 11:34:11 EDT

This article addresses a common misconception that old, unpaid debt automatically loses its power to harm financially after seven years. While the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) typically removes most negative items from credit reports after this period, it does not prevent debt collectors from pursuing legal action. To levy a bank account, a debt collector usually must first sue and obtain a court judgment, a process governed by state-specific statutes of limitations that can range from three to ten years. Crucially, making a partial payment or acknowledging an old debt can inadvertently restart this clock, making the debt legally enforceable again. Essential Wage Garnishment Rules for Borrowers to Understand Amid Record-High Debt

2026-03-19 11:14:12 EDT

This article details crucial wage garnishment rules that borrowers should understand, especially with credit card debt at record highs. Wage garnishment allows creditors to legally seize a portion of a paycheck, but federal law imposes strict limits, typically capping it at the lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or the amount exceeding 30 times the federal minimum wage. While most consumer debts require a court judgment for garnishment, exceptions exist for federal student loans, unpaid taxes, and child support, which can have higher limits. The article emphasizes that state laws can offer additional protections and that certain income types, like Social Security, are protected. It advises borrowers to act quickly upon receiving notice to dispute the debt or claim exemptions, as ignoring warnings can narrow options. (not investment advice)
