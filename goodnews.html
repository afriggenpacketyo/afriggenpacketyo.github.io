This article investigates the scientific understanding of oxytocin, commonly referred to as the "love hormone," and clarifies misconceptions about its function in human relationships. While initial research indicated oxytocin could enhance trust and communication in couples, subsequent studies struggled to replicate these results consistently. Neuroscientists now propose that oxytocin primarily intensifies existing social emotions, both favorable and unfavorable, rather than directly creating feelings of love or trust. The complex nature of its effects suggests it is not a straightforward "love potion" and can even exacerbate negative emotions like aggression or envy towards out-group individuals.
