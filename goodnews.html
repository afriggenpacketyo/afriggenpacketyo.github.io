The evolution of 'tree-hugger' from fringe term to badge of honor

2026-04-22 05:00:00 EDT

The term 'tree-hugger,' once a derogatory label for environmentalists, has transformed into a term of pride, reflecting a broader societal shift in environmental awareness. Originating from the Chipko movement in India in the 1970s, where villagers literally hugged trees to protect them from logging, the term initially carried connotations of grassroots activism and social justice. In the U.S., it emerged in the 1960s and gained traction in the 1990s as a dismissive label for environmental advocates. However, younger generations, like Gen Z, have embraced the term, associating it with ecofeminism and a deep connection to nature. The article highlights how the perception of environmentalism has evolved from a "weird fringe thing" to a mainstream concern.
