Understanding Hanukkah: History, Traditions, and Modern Celebrations

2025-12-12 09:54:00 EST

Hanukkah, often colloquially referred to as the "Jewish Christmas," is a significant Jewish Festival of Lights that commemorates the rededication of the Second Temple in Jerusalem around 200 BC. The eight-day celebration honors the miraculous event where a single jar of oil, intended to last for only one day, instead kept the menorah's candles burning for eight days following the Maccabean Revolt. During Hanukkah, a candle is traditionally lit each day on a nine-branched candelabrum, known as a hanukkiah, after nightfall. While historically not focused on gift-giving, the festival has become more commercialized over the years, with modern customs now including the exchange of gifts, especially among children, and the enjoyment of traditional deep-fried dishes like sufganiyot and latkes.
