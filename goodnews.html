Social Cues Drive Stripe Changes in Tomato Clownfish, Research Shows

2026-03-02 06:00:00 EST

New research published in PLOS Biology reveals that tomato clownfish alter their stripe patterns in response to social cues and habitat, a process driven by genetic and environmental factors. Unlike the three-striped Nemo clownfish, tomato clownfish lose most of their stripes as they mature, retaining only one. Experiments showed that the presence of adult clownfish on a live anemone significantly accelerates this stripe fading in juveniles. This adaptation is believed to help young fish integrate into the social hierarchy, signaling non-threat to older fish and avoiding eviction from vital anemone habitats.
