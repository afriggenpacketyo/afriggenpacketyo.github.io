California's election process, particularly its reliance on mail-in ballots, often leads to a slow and deliberate vote count, delaying the final determination of winners in close races. Examples like the 2022 L.A. mayoral primary and current contests for governor and L.A. mayor illustrate how early leads can evaporate as more ballots are processed. Experts explain that this system, while ensuring accuracy, can be frustrating for voters and politicians eager for definitive results. Late-breaking ballots, often favoring Democrats, can swing outcomes, making early projections unreliable. The article discusses the statistical probabilities for various races and the ongoing efforts to count and confirm all votes.

L.A. City Attorney faces likely ouster, mirroring historical trends

2026-06-05 06:00:00 EDT

Incumbent L.A. City Attorney Hydee Feldstein Soto is on the verge of being ousted in the primary, a rare event not seen in nearly a century. Her potential defeat, driven by challenger Marissa Roy's appeal to progressive voters and John McKinney's tough-on-crime platform, reflects themes reminiscent of Depression-era Los Angeles, with coalitions forming around workers' rights and anxieties about public safety. Analysts suggest Feldstein Soto's centrist position left her vulnerable in a race where many voters lacked detailed information. The outcome highlights the challenges of explaining the city attorney's role and the impact of campaign messaging and funding in shaping voter perception.
