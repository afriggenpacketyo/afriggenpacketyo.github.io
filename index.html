Dr. Mary Marfisee, a UCLA physician, is passionately dedicated to addressing the dire health outcomes faced by unhoused women on Los Angeles' Skid Row, where access to essential gynecological and prenatal care is severely limited. She notes that for these women, the immediate priority is finding stable housing, often leading to the neglect of health issues that then escalate. Marfisee emphasizes the unique challenges faced by homeless women, including significantly low rates of preventative screenings and systemic barriers in accessing consistent care. In response, she has spearheaded a new women's health initiative at the Union Rescue Mission, collaborating with a local hospital to launch a mobile health van. This innovative program aims to provide free pap smears and mammograms, with Marfisee optimistically pushing to overcome logistical hurdles and begin patient screenings by February.

Long-Term Health Impacts of LA Wildfires Begin to Emerge, Highlighting "Toxic Soup" Risks

2026-01-14 05:00:00 EST

The long-term health consequences of the Los Angeles wildfires are gradually becoming apparent, with emerging research revealing that the "toxic soup" of pollutants released from burning homes, plastics, and car batteries caused significant health problems. Scientists detected elevated levels of lead and arsenic in the air, even far from the fire zones, and found that dangerous gases like benzene, toluene, and carbon tetrachloride persisted indoors for weeks after the blazes subsided. Emergency room data recorded a 24% increase in respiratory issues and a striking 47% jump in heart attacks in the 90 days following the fires, alongside spikes in abnormal blood tests. This research underscores the critical need for improved protection measures, standardized cleanup protocols, and sustained funding for comprehensive post-disaster health studies to mitigate future risks.
