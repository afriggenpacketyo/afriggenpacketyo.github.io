Exploring the Historical Origins and Evolution of Daylight Saving Time

2026-03-06 18:35:19 EST

Daylight saving time, where clocks "spring forward" an hour, has a complex history with various attributed origins. While often linked to farmers, they didn't universally support its adoption. Benjamin Franklin's satirical essay in 1784 is sometimes cited, but the Philadelphia Franklin Institute credits New Zealand entomologist George Hudson, who desired more daylight for insect collecting. British builder William Willet also advocated for advancing clocks in 1907 to save energy and promote activity. The practice was first widely adopted by Germany in 1916 during World War I to conserve fuel, with the U.S. following in 1918 before repealing it in 1919. It became year-round during WWII, and the Uniform Time Act of 1966 established the current system.
