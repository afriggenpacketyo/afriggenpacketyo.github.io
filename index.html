Microsoft's stock experienced its biggest daily decline since 2020, tumbling 10% after its second-quarter earnings report, despite beating analyst revenue expectations. Investors reacted negatively to the cloud computing platform Azure's growth, which came in slightly below consensus at 39%, compared to 40% in the previous quarter, and to a lower implied operating margin forecast for the third quarter. The company's CFO indicated that Azure's growth could have been higher if more data center infrastructure had been allocated to customers rather than prioritizing in-house AI needs and R&D efforts. Analysts suggest investors focused on this slight deceleration in Azure growth, indicating a shift in focus towards the company's long-term AI strategy rather than short-term stock performance.
