The United States and Bangladesh have unveiled a new trade agreement, establishing preferential, zero-tariff treatment for certain volumes of Bangladeshi textile and apparel imports into the US. This favorable treatment is reciprocally linked to the volume of US-produced cotton and man-made fiber exported to Bangladesh. While most Bangladeshi products entering the US market will still face a flat rate of 19%, a slight reduction from the previous 20%, the deal includes duty-free access for Bangladeshi-made ingredients for pharmaceuticals and aircraft parts. In return, Bangladesh has committed to providing significant preferential market access for a wide range of US industrial and agricultural goods, including chemicals, medical devices, and farm products, with tariffs on items like poultry and corn reduced to zero. This agreement marks a meaningful step forward in opening markets and creating new opportunities for American exporters. (not investment advice)

Los Angeles Seeks to Remove Federal Judge Overseeing Homelessness Settlement, Citing Overreach

2026-02-09 20:09:39 EST

The City of Los Angeles has formally requested an appeals court to remove U.S. District Judge David O. Carter from a critical homelessness settlement case, alleging he has repeatedly overstepped his judicial authority. Attorneys for the city claim Carter has used his bench as a "bully pulpit" to dictate the city's budget and homelessness policy, making a "litany of errors" and threatening aggressive actions. This contentious move comes ahead of a hearing scheduled to explore whether the city misled the federal court regarding its plan to clear thousands of homelessness encampments. The plaintiffs, the LA Alliance for Human Rights, counter that the city is attempting to avoid accountability for its alleged failures to comply with the 2022 settlement and its obligations to accurately report on progress, further prolonging the legal proceedings over a pressing social issue.
