2025-05-17 16:38:36 EDT

Paleontologists have unearthed the fossilized remains of a 500-million-year-old, three-eyed predator named Mosura fentoni, also known as the "sea moth", in the Burgess Shale of the Canadian Rockies. This creature, part of the extinct group radiodonts, possessed a unique pencil sharpener-like mouth and intimidating claws, challenging previous understandings of radiodont complexity. The discovery revealed a multi-segmented abdomen-like region with gills, similar to modern arthropods, and provided insights into the open circulatory system of this ancient predator. The sea moth's existence sheds light on the diversity of life during the Cambrian period and the rapid evolution of animal groups.
