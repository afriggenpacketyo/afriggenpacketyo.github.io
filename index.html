A woman who experienced childhood abuse by her mother is advocating for legal changes to revoke parental rights for abusive parents, even when they are incarcerated. Kelly Higgins, whose mother was jailed for her role in a child murder, highlights the ongoing trauma caused by abusive parents retaining some rights, such as approving holidays or piercings. Higgins argues that such visitation rights constitute continued manipulation and abuse. While the Ministry of Justice states child protection is a priority and has enacted some restrictions on parental responsibility in severe cases, Higgins believes these changes are insufficient. She advocates for a transfer of parental rights to foster parents to provide greater stability and healing for children.

Britain risks AI dependency on US tech giants, warns minister

2026-04-29 01:00:46 EDT

A British minister has warned that the UK risks becoming dependent on a handful of U.S. tech giants for artificial intelligence, the 'currency of the future.' Liz Kendall argues that this reliance could undermine Britain's economic, scientific, and military advantages. She calls for cooperation among 'middle powers' like democracies in Europe, Japan, and South Korea to develop a resilient digital ecosystem independent of 'powerful, unaccountable few.' This concern is amplified by the volatile U.S. political climate and the growing technological gap between the U.S. and China. The article also touches on the ethical considerations of AI development, the potential for AI as a weapon, and the complex geopolitical landscape surrounding technology.

Home Office recognizes Al Fayed abuse survivor as victim of modern slavery

2026-04-29 01:00:12 EDT

The Home Office has officially recognized Rachael Louw, a survivor of abuse by Mohamed Al Fayed, as a victim of modern slavery. This marks the first time the British government has formally acknowledged such a status for a victim in this case. Louw, who was trafficked and abused by Al Fayed and his brother Salah, described the decision as 'vindication and validation.' This ruling is seen as a significant step towards accountability and may influence ongoing investigations and potential criminal proceedings. At least three other women linked to Al Fayed are reportedly awaiting similar rulings, signaling a broader recognition of modern slavery within these historical cases.
