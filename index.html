Behavioral Economics Explains 'The Winner's Curse' in Auctions and Bidding Wars

The concept of "The Winner's Curse," popularized by Nobel laureate Richard Thaler, describes a common phenomenon in competitive bidding where the winner often overpays for an item. This anomaly challenges traditional economic models by suggesting that the winner of an auction is frequently the person who most overestimates an item's value, not the one who values it most rationally. The curse has been observed in various markets, from oil drilling rights to corporate acquisitions and professional sports drafts. To avoid it, Thaler advises bidders to consider whether they would still be happy with their purchase if they knew they had placed the highest bid among many.
