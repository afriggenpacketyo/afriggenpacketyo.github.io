Lord Wolfson, CEO of the retail company Next, has warned of a "dramatic" decline in entry-level job opportunities, citing a doubling of applicants per shop vacancy compared to two years ago. He attributes this crisis in youth unemployment to broader economic issues and upcoming changes in employment law, which he believes will make hiring more difficult. Wolfson also called for the government to reverse increases in National Insurance contributions and minimum wage rates, arguing that economic growth is the primary solution. Latest figures show the youth unemployment rate at 16.2%, the highest since 2014. While Next's online business is thriving, the company has reduced staff in physical stores due to rising costs. The government, however, defends its employment policies, highlighting a £2.5 billion youth employment support package and measures to ensure staff security.

Berlin Hauptbahnhof celebrates 20 years as a symbolic hub of reunification

2026-05-26 02:57:00 EDT

Berlin Hauptbahnhof, Germany's central train station, is celebrating its 20th anniversary, marking two decades since its inauguration as a symbol of German reunification. Built on a site that was once part of the Berlin Wall's no-man's-land, the station's design by Meinhard von Gerkan aimed to physically and symbolically reconnect the divided city. Its multi-level structure, characterized by steel and glass, was envisioned as a "cathedral of transport" and aligned with the Reichstag's dome, representing transparency and unity. Despite cost-cutting modifications to the original architectural plans, which led to a copyright dispute, the station has become a vital transport hub, serving approximately 1,800 trains and over 300,000 passengers daily. It is now a prominent modern landmark, embodying the spirit of a reunified Berlin and Germany.

Ebola outbreak in Congo hampered by community distrust and attacks on aid workers

2026-05-26 02:46:25 EDT

The fight against a rare Bundibugyo type of Ebola outbreak in eastern Congo is being severely complicated by community distrust and direct attacks on aid workers. Volunteers like Vanny Birungi face hostility, including being pelted with stones, due to suspicion and misinformation, with some residents believing Ebola is a "white man's invention." Healthcare facilities have been attacked, leading to the evacuation of patients and the burning of treatment centers, exacerbating the spread of the virus. The lack of trust, amplified by armed conflict and past aid cuts, makes containment efforts extremely challenging. The outbreak has neared 1,000 suspected cases, but health centers are being destroyed, and responders are underprepared and underprotected. Experts emphasize that community engagement and trust are as critical as the health response itself to effectively combat the epidemic.
