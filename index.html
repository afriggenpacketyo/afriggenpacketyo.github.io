The widespread observance of January 1st as New Year's Day is rooted in the ancient Roman calendar and the subsequent adoption of the Gregorian calendar. Initially a 10-month system, the Roman calendar was expanded to 12 months, with January named after Janus, the god of beginnings. Julius Caesar further refined the calendar to 365 days with leap years, though minor inaccuracies led Pope Gregory XIII to introduce the Gregorian calendar in 1582, which became globally adopted due to European influence. While many cultures observe January 1st for practical purposes, numerous others celebrate their own New Year's based on lunar or other calendars, such as the Chinese Lunar New Year and Rosh Hashanah.
