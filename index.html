The question of whether it is morally wrong to have a child when poor is explored, with experts arguing against the idea that a specific financial threshold must be met for responsible reproduction. The article traces the historical roots of this notion to concepts like the "deserving poor" and Malthusian economics, which blamed poverty on individual reproductive choices. It asserts that reproductive freedom should not be a privilege earned through financial self-sufficiency, but a fundamental right. The piece emphasizes that societal structures, not just individual choices, contribute to poverty, and that communities have obligations to support families. Ultimately, the article suggests that love, attention, and social support are invaluable, and that parents who raise children under hardship may be demonstrating bravery rather than irresponsibility.

Rogie Vachon: From Farm Boy to LA's First Hockey Star

2026-04-12 07:00:00 EDT

Rogie Vachon, the first hockey star of Los Angeles, reminisces about his transition from a small-town farm in Quebec to the vibrant, sun-drenched world of Southern California. Traded to the Kings in 1971, Vachon embraced a new style, complete with long hair and a mustache, which resonated with the Hollywood culture and helped him connect with fans. He led the Kings to unprecedented success, including nine consecutive playoff appearances, a franchise record. Vachon's impact extended beyond playing; he later returned as general manager, orchestrating the trade for Wayne Gretzky and drafting Hall of Famers. Now retired on an eight-acre ranch in Montana, Vachon enjoys a peaceful life surrounded by animals, reflecting on a career that helped save hockey in Southern California.
