Understanding the Consequences of a Debt Write-Off for Borrowers

2025-11-07 13:08:50 EST

When a creditor writes off a debt, it does not mean the obligation is forgiven; rather, it signifies the lender is classifying it as a loss for accounting purposes after about 180 days of non-payment. Following a write-off, the debt is typically sold to a collection agency, which will then pursue the borrower for the full amount, often with added fees and interest. This action severely damages a person's credit score, as the charge-off can remain on their credit report for up to seven years. Borrowers may still face lawsuits and could even owe taxes on any portion of the debt that is eventually forgiven.

Understanding the Rules for Debt Collectors Adding Interest and Fees

2025-11-07 12:31:18 EST

Debt collectors are generally permitted to add interest and fees to an unpaid debt, but only if such charges were stipulated in the original contract with the creditor. They cannot arbitrarily invent new fees or raise interest rates beyond what the initial agreement or state law allows. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) provides consumer protection by requiring collectors to provide a detailed validation notice of the debt, including any added charges. If a borrower disputes the debt, the collector must verify the amount and justify any additional costs, facing penalties for falsely inflating a balance.
