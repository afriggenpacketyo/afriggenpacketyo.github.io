History and origins of Daylight Saving Time explained

2026-03-06 18:35:19 EST

Daylight Saving Time (DST), which involves "springing forward" an hour this Sunday, has a complex history with various figures and motivations attributed to its origin. While often associated with farmers, they did not necessarily support the practice when it was adopted. Benjamin Franklin is sometimes credited with a satirical essay proposing early rising, but modern DST is more closely linked to George Hudson, a New Zealand entomologist, and William Willet, a British builder, who advocated for more daylight hours. Germany first implemented DST during World War I to conserve fuel, with the U.S. following suit in 1918. After being repealed, DST was reinstated during World War II and later standardized by the Uniform Time Act of 1966, though its year-round observance was attempted again in response to the 1973 oil embargo.

British number one Emma Raducanu began her Indian Wells campaign with a dominant straight-sets victory over Anastasia Zakharova, winning 6-1, 6-3. Raducanu expressed satisfaction with her performance, stating her game was in a "really good place" and that the win provided confidence in her training. She highlighted her clean ball-striking and aggressive baseline play. Raducanu is set to face world number six Amanda Anisimova in the third round, provided Anisimova wins her upcoming match. This strong start follows recent mixed results for Raducanu, including a second-round exit at the Australian Open and reaching a final in Cluj.
