Understanding the Consequences of Having Debt Written Off by Creditors

2025-11-07 13:08:50 EST

When a creditor writes off a debt, it does not mean the borrower is no longer responsible for the balance; rather, it signifies an accounting change for the lender. Typically occurring after 180 days of non-payment, a write-off results in the account being reported as a "charge-off," which can severely damage a person's credit score for up to seven years. The original creditor often sells the debt to a collection agency, which will then pursue the borrower for the full amount, potentially with added fees and interest. To avoid this stage, experts suggest exploring debt relief options like settlement or consolidation before an account becomes severely delinquent.

Understanding a Debt Collector's Ability to Add Interest and Fees to Unpaid Balances

2025-11-07 12:31:18 EST

Debt collectors are generally permitted to add interest and fees to an unpaid debt, but only if such charges were stipulated in the original contract with the creditor or are allowed by state law. They cannot arbitrarily invent new fees or increase interest rates beyond what was legally agreed upon. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) protects consumers by requiring collectors to provide a detailed validation notice and justification for all charges if the debt is disputed.
