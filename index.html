Roman King Numa Pompilius extended the calendar to 12 months, adding January, named after Janus, the god of beginnings, and establishing January 1st as the start. Julius Caesar later refined this with a 365-day calendar and leap years, which Pope Gregory XIII further corrected in 1582, creating the Gregorian calendar. The article explains that while many cultures use other dates for their new year celebrations, such as the Lunar New Year or Rosh Hashanah, January 1st has become globally prevalent due to European colonization. Psychologically, people gravitate to New Year's Day for a "fresh start effect," perceiving it as a chapter break for new beginnings and optimism. The article suggests that while January 1st is the most popular, any day can serve as a fresh start, emphasizing the importance of small, enjoyable, and actionable goals for sticking to resolutions.
