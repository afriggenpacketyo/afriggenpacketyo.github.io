Explanation of the Consequences When a Creditor Writes Off Unpaid Debt

2025-11-07 13:08:50 EST

This article clarifies that a "debt write-off" by a creditor does not mean the borrower's obligation is forgiven or erased. Instead, it is an accounting measure where the original lender marks the debt as uncollectible, often after about 180 days of non-payment. The debt is then typically sold to a collection agency, which will pursue the full amount, and the charge-off remains on the consumer's credit report for up to seven years, significantly damaging their credit score.

Analysis of Whether Debt Collectors Can Legally Add Interest and Fees to Unpaid Balances

2025-11-07 12:31:18 EST

This article addresses whether it is legal for debt collection agencies to add interest and fees to an outstanding debt. Generally, collectors can do so only if the original credit agreement signed by the borrower allowed for such charges to continue accruing after default. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) and various state laws place limits on these additions, prohibiting collectors from imposing arbitrary fees not specified in the original contract. Consumers are protected and have the right to dispute and demand verification of any added charges.
