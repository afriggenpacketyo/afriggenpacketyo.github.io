While Lyons denied the existence of a specific "protester database" or a "domestic terrorist" database, the letter acknowledges collecting "essential biographic and biometric information and situational details" on individuals involved in protests where alleged criminal conduct occurred. Civil liberties experts view this as a significant acknowledgement of federal agencies collecting and preserving information on protesters and observers, raising concerns about potential First Amendment violations and the use of surveillance tools.
